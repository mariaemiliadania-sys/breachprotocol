// SUBJECT 02 — MARCUS WEBB
// SUNDAY NIGHT — 22:34
Marcus Webb
IT Systems Administrator — 7 years at Meridian Global
Marcus is one of the most trusted people in the building. He has access to every system — that's his job. After 7 years, nobody questions what he does or when he does it.

Sunday night, he sets up a scheduled maintenance script before leaving at 10:30pm. He's done it dozens of times. The script runs automatically under his credentials.

What Marcus doesn't know — or does he? — is that someone has been watching his patterns for weeks. And tonight, his unmonitored, unrestricted access becomes the entry point for everything that follows.
When did you last review who has unrestricted access in your organization?

// THE DILEMMA
You manage IT policy.
What do you change?
Marcus's situation was made possible by how your organization handles access. Which problem do you fix first?
A
Require two-factor authentication for all remote access. Even with valid credentials, a second verification step would have flagged — or blocked — the remote session after Marcus left the building.
B
Review and limit privileged access regularly. No one should have unrestricted access to every system indefinitely. Even trusted employees should have access reviewed annually — and scoped to what they actually need.
C
Set up automated alerts for after-hours activity. Any system access outside of normal working hours — especially with admin credentials — should trigger an automatic notification to a security contact.

// THE LESSON
Trust is not a security strategy.
Marcus had 7 years of trust. And that trust — without controls — became a vulnerability.

In security, the question is never "do we trust this person?" The question is: "What happens if this person's account is compromised? What happens if they make a mistake? What happens if they leave?"

The goal is not to distrust people. The goal is to build systems where even trusted people can't cause catastrophic damage — accidentally or otherwise.
"The most dangerous access is the access nobody questions — because it's always been there."
