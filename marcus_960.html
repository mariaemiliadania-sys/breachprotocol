Marcus is one of the most trusted people in the building. He has access to every system — that's his job. After 7 years, nobody questions what he does or when.

Sunday night, he sets up a scheduled maintenance script before leaving at 10:30pm. He's done it dozens of times. What Marcus doesn't know — or does he? — is that his unmonitored, unrestricted access becomes the entry point for everything that follows.
When did you last review who has unrestricted access in your organization?
// SUBJECT 02 — MARCUS WEBB
// THE DILEMMA
You manage IT policy. What do you change?
Marcus's situation was made possible by how your organization handles access. Which problem do you fix first?
A
Require two-factor authentication for all remote access. Even with valid credentials, a second verification step would have flagged — or blocked — the remote session after Marcus left the building.
B
Review and limit privileged access regularly. No one should have unrestricted access to every system indefinitely. Even trusted employees should have access reviewed annually — scoped to what they actually need.
C
Set up automated alerts for after-hours activity. Any system access outside normal working hours — especially with admin credentials — should trigger an automatic notification to a security contact.

// THE LESSON
Trust is not a security strategy.
Marcus had 7 years of trust. And that trust — without controls — became a vulnerability.

In security, the question is never "do we trust this person?" The question is: "What happens if this person's account is compromised? What if they make a mistake? What if they leave?"

The goal is not to distrust people. The goal is to build systems where even trusted people can't cause catastrophic damage — accidentally or otherwise.
"The most dangerous access is the access nobody questions — because it's always been there."
// ALL THREE OPTIONS ADDRESS REAL VULNERABILITIES
