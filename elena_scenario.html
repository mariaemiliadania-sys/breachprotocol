// SUBJECT 01 — ELENA VASQUEZ
// SUNDAY NIGHT — 22:47
Elena Vasquez
Senior Data Analyst — 4 years at Meridian Global
It's Sunday night. Elena is still working — the board presentation is Monday morning and her manager asked for a full client portfolio breakdown.

The office system is slow. She's frustrated. She plugs in her personal USB drive to copy the files and finish the work from home.

She's done this before. It saves time. Nobody ever said anything.

She copies 40,000 client records, drives home, and falls asleep thinking about Monday's presentation.
Sound familiar? This happens in organizations every day.
// SUBJECT 01 — ELENA VASQUEZ
// THE DILEMMA
You're Elena.
What do you do?
The system is slow, the deadline is real, and the USB is right there.
A
Use the USB. You have a deadline. The data is for a legitimate work purpose. You'll delete it from the drive once the presentation is done. It's a one-time thing.
B
Contact IT first. Ask them to fix the VPN or give you an authorized way to access the files remotely. It might take longer, but you follow the process.
C
Tell your manager. Explain the situation and let them decide how to handle it. The deadline pressure came from above — the decision should too.

// SUBJECT 01 — ELENA VASQUEZ
// THE LESSON
Good intentions don't protect data.
Elena didn't mean to cause a breach. She was trying to do her job well, under real pressure, with a tool that was immediately available.

But intent doesn't matter once data leaves a secure environment. A personal USB drive, a personal email, a photo on a phone — any of these can become a breach, regardless of why the data was moved.

The problem isn't Elena. The problem is a system that made the wrong thing easier than the right thing.
"If doing the right thing is harder than doing the wrong thing, people will do the wrong thing — even with the best intentions."
