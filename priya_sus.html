Priya is working late on a regulatory audit. A new regulation requires her to understand how client data is categorized — and her current access doesn't cover financial records.

So she approves her own access exception. She's done it before for audits. It's within her authority. She reviews the records, documents her findings, and logs out at 10:30pm — 17 minutes before the breach occurs.

Priya did nothing wrong that night. But her access exception set a precedent that nobody questioned.
Who is responsible when a process works exactly as designed — but the design has a flaw?

You're reviewing the incident. Where does the responsibility lie?

A
Priya should not have approved her own access exception. Even if it was within her authority, self-approval creates a conflict of interest. A second sign-off should always be required.

// THE LESSON
A process that works is not the same as a process that's right.
Priya followed every rule. The exception was approved, documented, and justified. And yet, the same mechanism that let her do her job legitimately could have been exploited by anyone with the same access.

Security processes need to be reviewed — not just followed. A policy that made sense three years ago may create a vulnerability today.

The question is not just "did we follow the process?" but "is our process still appropriate for the risks we face today?"
"The most dangerous policies are the ones everyone follows without questioning whether they still make sense."
